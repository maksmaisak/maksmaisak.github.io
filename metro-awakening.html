AI Architecture

Screenshot: HTN graph, Gameplay Debugger, or Visual Logger view that helps explain the architecture.
images/metro_awakening/htn_architecture.png

Enemy AI had two layers. A state machine handled high-level modes — Unaware, Investigate, Alarmed, Combat — while my HTN plugin chose concrete actions inside each: investigating sounds, moving to cover, advancing, shooting, suppressing, retreating.

All archetypes shared a token system, group communication, location reservation, and designer-facing parameter templates.

Perception

Video (8-12s): select a human AI, enable sight field debug drawing. Show Focus, Normal, and Periphery cones tracking the player, with trace lines changing color as awareness builds.
images/metro_awakening/perception_sight_fields.mp4

Unreal's AIPerception didn't meet the project's performance or flexibility needs, so I built a custom perception framework with time-sliced updates viable on Quest 2.

Sight ran through multiple prioritized fields with per-target awareness rates and darkness-aware detection.

Hearing propagated noise through the navmesh with material-based dampening.

Additional sensors covered projectile fly-bys and dead bodies, each with Visual Logger debug views.

Cover and Human Combat

Editor screenshot: show auto-generated cover points along navmesh edges. Enable cover point visualization, position camera for wide shot showing low cover (one color) and high cover (another) with line-of-fire indicators.
images/metro_awakening/cover_system_overview.png

The cover system automatically generated points from navmesh edges, classified each as low or high cover, and scored them by body coverage. That data fed EQS queries so enemies could reason about actual protection.

Video (10-15s): enable cover point safety visualization and walk the player around a level with enemies. Points near the player and enemies should visibly update (flash/change color) more often than distant ones. Show green (safe) turning red (unsafe) as the player moves to flank.
images/metro_awakening/cover_dynamic_safety.mp4

Static cover data was generated offline. CoverTrackers then kept safety up to date against live threats, so enemies reacted when the player flanked or disappeared. Updates ran within a 0.1 ms per-frame budget on Quest 2, with nearby points refreshing more often than distant ones.

Video (10-15s): human enemies in cover-based combat. Show popping in and out of cover, shooting, and at least one enemy repositioning when flanked.
images/metro_awakening/enemy_human_combat.mp4

Humans fought from cover, repositioned when exposed, and selected positions through EQS based on safety and the player's location. Token-based coordination stopped squads from crowding one spot or all firing at once.

An accuracy system and suppressive fire let designers control pressure without overwhelming the player.

Other Enemies

Video (10-15s): nosalises in combat, surrounding the player and attacking in a group. If possible, enable combat circle debug visualization during part of the clip.
images/metro_awakening/enemy_nosalis.mp4

Nosalises

Nosalises used a Combat Circle that distributed attackers into obstacle-aware sectors around the player, forming natural surrounds in open areas and semicircles in tight spaces. On top of that: telegraphed attacks, aim avoidance when the player looked at them, and weakspot-driven staggers.

Video (10-15s): lurkers hiding, attacking, and retreating to new hiding spots.
images/metro_awakening/enemy_lurker.mp4

Lurkers

Lurkers hid in dark spots, attacked in coordinated waves (bounce, drive-by, and proximity attacks), then retreated to new hiding spots. They scattered when allies died nearby.

Video (10-15s): hunters using hit-and-run tactics, hiding, dodging, and pair attacks.
images/metro_awakening/enemy_hunter.mp4

Hunters

Hunters were stealthy blowgun enemies that picked hiding spots with real line of sight, dodged toward their next destination when shot at, and attacked in pairs from different angles if the player stayed still too long.

Collision Avoidance

Video (8-12s): multiple characters navigating past each other in a corridor or tight space. Enable debug visualization showing candidate avoidance directions and navmesh edge clipping.
images/metro_awakening/rvo_avoidance.mp4

Unreal's avoidance assumed directly controlled velocity and had no navmesh boundary awareness — a bad fit for root-motion combat.

I rewrote UAvoidanceManager with navmesh-aware candidate directions, animation-driven velocity, distinct handling of moving vs. stationary characters, and a fallback that minimized penetration depth when collision was unavoidable.

Damage Response

Video (8-12s): shoot enemies in different body parts. Show directional hit reactions and full stagger when damage threshold is reached.
images/metro_awakening/stagger_hit_reactions.mp4

The system separated light hit reactions (additive flinches) from full staggers (interruptive), selected by hit direction, body part, and damage type. All selection was data-driven so designers could tune responses without code changes.

Functional Testing

Screenshot: Test Automation window with AI-related tests listed and passing.
images/metro_awakening/functional_testing.png

I wrote 50+ automated functional tests covering perception, state transitions, cover selection, combat coordination, and character pooling. Regressions were caught before they reached QA, and late-stage tuning was safer because of it.

Performance

Screenshot: Unreal Insights, stat capture, or another profiling view showing AI-side performance investigation or improvement work.
images/metro_awakening/performance_profiling.png

The AI had to hold up on Quest 2, so performance work happened alongside feature work: time-slicing perception and cover updates, porting hot-path Blueprint logic to C++, integrating the Animation Budget Allocator, and profiling-driven fixes like eliminating stale references that caused 120× more path recalculations than necessary.
