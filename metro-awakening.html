AI Architecture

Screenshot: HTN graph, Gameplay Debugger, or Visual Logger view that helps explain the architecture.
images/metro_awakening/htn_architecture.jpg

Enemy AI had two layers. A lightweight awareness state machine handled high-level states such as Unaware, Startled, Investigate, Alarmed, and Combat.

Inside each state, my Hierarchical Task Networks plugin chose concrete actions such as investigating a noise, searching cover near the last known position, advancing, shooting, suppressing, retreating, or triggering archetype-specific behavior.

The structure was supported by shared systems for AI tokens, group communication, location reservation, and parameter templates.

Perception

Editor screenshot: select a human AI character, enable "Show > Impact > Show Sight Fields: Of Selected Actor". Show coffin-shaped and cone-shaped sight fields (Focus, Normal, Periphery) with Metro architecture in background.
images/metro_awakening/perception_sight_fields.jpg

Unreal's built-in AIPerception did not fit the project's performance and flexibility needs, so I built a custom time-sliced perception framework. Sensor work was budgeted across frames to make it viable on Quest 2-class standalone VR hardware.

  • Sight: multiple prioritized sight fields, awareness rates tied to what was seen, and darkness-aware detection
  • Hearing: navmesh-based noise propagation with obstacle dampening so stealth felt readable and grounded in level geometry
  • Custom sensors: projectile fly-bys, dead-body detection, and matching debug views in the Visual Logger
Video (8-12s): enable sight field debug drawing, then show the player stepping from a dark area into light (enemy detects), then back into darkness (enemy loses sight). Sight trace lines changing color sell this well.
images/metro_awakening/perception_darkness.mp4

Metro's stealth depends on players understanding why they were or were not seen. The darkness integration made light, cover, and line of sight consistent and readable.

Cover and Human Combat

Editor screenshot: show auto-generated cover points along navmesh edges. Enable cover point visualization, position camera for wide shot showing low cover (one color) and high cover (another) with line-of-fire indicators.
images/metro_awakening/cover_system_overview.jpg

Human combat was built around a cover system that automatcally generated points from navmesh edges, classified them as low or high cover, and scored them by body coverage. That data fed EQS queries and level-design controls, so enemies could reason about actual protection.

Video (10-15s): enable cover point safety visualization and walk the player around a level with enemies. Points near the player and enemies should visibly update (flash/change color) more often than distant ones. Show green (safe) turning red (unsafe) as the player moves to flank.
images/metro_awakening/cover_dynamic_safety.mp4

Static cover data was generated offline. CoverTrackers kept runtime safety up to date against live threats so enemies could react when the player flanked, advanced, or disappeared. Updates ran within a 0.1ms per-frame budget on Quest 2, with nearby points refreshing more often than distant ones.

Video (10-15s): human enemies in cover-based combat. Show popping in and out of cover, shooting, and at least one enemy repositioning when flanked.
images/metro_awakening/enemy_human_combat.mp4

Humans could fight from cover, reposition when exposed, and maintain pressure. They selected cover through EQS, used safer routes to get there, and switched behavior when the player's position changed.

Token-based coordination and location reservation stopped squads from dogpiling the same spot or all firing at once.

An accuracy system and suppressive fire let designers keep pressure on the player without overwhelming them.

Other Enemies

Video (10-15s): nosalises in combat, surrounding the player and attacking in a group. If possible, enable combat circle debug visualization during part of the clip.
images/metro_awakening/enemy_nosalis.mp4

Nosalises

Nosalises were built around pressure and player positioning. Their key system was the Combat Circle, which distributed melee enemies into obstacle-aware sectors around the player so they could surround naturally in open areas and form semicircles in tighter spaces.

Their behavior stack handled telegraphs, attacks, landings, noise escalation, and aim avoidance.

Video (10-15s): lurkers hiding, attacking, and retreating to new hiding spots.
images/metro_awakening/enemy_lurker.mp4

Lurkers

Lurkers were built around hide-attack-retreat loops instead of constant exposure. They used bounce, drive-by, and proximity attacks, coordinated into waves, and scattered when allies died.

Video (10-15s): hunters using hit-and-run tactics, hiding, dodging, and pair attacks.
images/metro_awakening/enemy_hunter.mp4

Hunters

Hunters were stealthy blowgun enemies built around ambush positions, evasive movement, and pair attacks. They preferred hiding spots with real line of sight, dodged in purposeful directions, and retreated back into cover between attacks.

Collision Avoidance

Video (8-12s): multiple characters navigating past each other in a corridor or tight space. Enable debug visualization showing candidate avoidance directions and navmesh edge clipping.
images/metro_awakening/rvo_avoidance.mp4

Unreal's built-in avoidance was not good enough for the project's root-motion-driven characters. It assumed directly controlled velocity and had no real awareness of navmesh boundaries, which made it a bad fit for combat movement.

I rewrote the engine's UAvoidanceManager to use navmesh-aware candidate directions, actual animation-driven velocity, better handling of moving and stationary characters, and a fallback that minimized collision depth when avoidance was unavoidable.

Damage Response

Video (8-12s): shoot enemies in different body parts. Show directional hit reactions and full stagger when damage threshold is reached.
images/metro_awakening/stagger_hit_reactions.mp4

Damage response needed to communicate impact without turning every hit into a hard stun. The system separated light hit reactions from full staggers, with data-driven selection based on hit direction, hit location, and damage type. That kept combat feedback readable and tuning in data instead of hard-coded cases.
